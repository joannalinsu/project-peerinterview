12 years ago, Ike Wilson, the born and raised Californian, came to the east coast as a freshman at Yale, studying French. This "far-away from home" Ivy-league college lured him, whose childhood dream had always been going to a school in Cal.

Ike has a long story with language. The last 30 years, he jumps from one language to another, all for some reasons.

Born in a Vietnamese-American family, Ike spent most of his childhood in the vietnamese-speaking environment- mostly came from his mother and grandparents. "After age five, my mom got busy and I spent more time with my dad. I can still understand, but can't speak anymore now," he said.

Unlike many of the second or third immigrant generations in the states, Ike thought that losing his connection to Vietnamese is "embarrasing, personally". "The first reason is that I took pride of the process of learning language, and another is that my grandfather, on my mother side, was the author of English-Vietnamese dictionary, who went to U.S with F1 visa and worked in D.C for government and embassy." Moreover, his gradfather on the other side was a Japanese historian.

Although he has only been to Vietnam once, he was quite confident about regaining this language, saying that "if you kidnap me to Vietnam for three months, I could pick that up".

After that, he went to a French school in his neighborhood. "I learned everything in French school," he said. Before vietnam war broke out, this southeast-asia country was conquered by French. Ike's mother, who was born in the States but raised in Vietnam, went to French school there. "My monther thought French school is better than the U.S ones, and also I experienced that," Ike explained.

Later, like a typical american teenager, he went to high school in English environment and minored in Spanish. It was not until his trip to Yale did he regain his French. "It was like a personal challenge- I wanted to be completely fluent in that language again," he said. Just like many hardworking 22-year-old junior, he went study abroad at Paris. "I wanted to do study abroad in Paris for my whole life," Ike was exited about this, "but I regret that I spent too much time in proving myself that I can regain this language."

But efforts paid off when he first started his educational tech career in NYC. Beside editing content for the company, he also helped in building its French version app. "It was quite fulfilling in my first job", Ike said.

“I didn’t plan to stay in the East coast,” Ike explained, “but New York traps you, better or worse. I am ok and aware of it.” He moved to NYC, in which most of his friends are, dived into education tech industry after graduation, and now changing his career path to a broader concept of "content production". "I want to do content for a wider audience and in a wider range", Ike confidently said.
